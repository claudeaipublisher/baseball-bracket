Scout analysis

The walk rate is the defining issue. 15 walks in 9.1 innings — 1.61 per inning — means Paul is putting the leadoff runner on base in nearly every inning he pitches. The Feb 21 outing is the starkest example: 4 walks in 1.0 inning with only 12 strikes out of 30 pitches, yet zero hits allowed. The offense did not need a single hit to score against him that day.

Strike% of 50.0% is right at the floor of viability. He has not cleared 60% in any appearance. The variance is the other concern — swinging from 40.0% to 59.1% game to game tells you the delivery is not yet repeatable. When he is at 59.1% he looks like a different pitcher. That game (Apr 16) was also his cleanest across all four indicators: 1 BB, 0 WP, 2 K in 1.0 IP.

The wild pitch problem appears to be resolving. Five WP across the first three games, zero in the last two. Back-to-back clean WP outings in Apr 16 and Apr 18 — including his worst ERA performance — suggests a mechanical adjustment may have been made. The WP improvement held even when everything else fell apart against Brew Crew.

The strikeout arm is real and should not be overlooked. 16 K in 9.1 innings (1.72 per inning) gives him genuine upside. His best game was Mar 31 — 6 K in 3.0 IP — showing he can miss bats in volume when he is on. K/BB has also trended upward across the five games from 0.50 to a range of 1.20–2.00 in the three most recent outings. The Apr 18 blowup came from contact (5 H, 7 ER), not walks.

Development focus — First-pitch strike rate is the single most important metric to track. Getting ahead in the count immediately reduces walk risk, reduces pitch count, and allows him to use his strikeout stuff in favorable counts rather than falling behind. The WP trend is encouraging and suggests he is coachable. The ceiling here is meaningful if the Strike% stabilizes above 55%.

The STORM 12U outing is the defining data point. In 2.0 innings against a mid-upper tier opponent, Tripp issued 6 walks and threw 4 wild pitches. ERA of 4.91 is the most misleading number in this profile. Two earned runs in 3.2 innings looks like a success story until you see 8 walks and 5 wild pitches in the same sample. The strikeout data shows genuine arm talent. 5 K in 3.2 IP, including 3 in his best outing (2.0 IP vs STORM). When Tripp is challenging hitters he can miss bats. The problem is the ratio of free baserunners to strikeouts. He has 8 walks and 5 wild pitches (13 free events) against 5 strikeouts. The giveback rate far exceeds the takeaway rate.

Strike% of 53% is directional only given the limited pitch data available. It sits just below the 55% target and is consistent with a pitcher who is competitive but not yet in command of the strike zone.

Development focus — Wild pitches are the most urgent area because they are the most controllable through mechanical adjustment. With 3.2 IP and only one game against quality opposition, the sample is too small to know how much of the STORM outing was circumstance versus persistent tendency. More outings against mid-tier or better opponents are needed.

The shared command signature is unmistakable. The K/BB of 0.91 just below 1.0 tells the clearest story — for every strikeout recorded, there is nearly one walk issued. The offense is getting free baserunners at almost the same rate the pitcher is getting outs through his best weapon.

The combined profile is a walk-and-wild-pitch machine. 23 walks plus 10 wild pitches across 13.0 innings means the combined pitcher produces 33 free baserunner-advancement events in 39 outs recorded. That is 0.85 free events per out — nearly one free gift for every out they get. At 12U, where baserunners score at a high rate regardless of the quality of the hit, this is an unsustainable profile against competitive opposition.

Where they differ matters for coaching. Paul’s WP/IP has improved to 0.00 over his last two games while Tripp’s WP problem was concentrated against his toughest opponent. Paul’s K/BB is trending upward (0.50 early, 1.20–2.00 recently). Tripp’s ERA looks better because he has faced fewer quality opponents and allowed less contact. These are different expression points of the same underlying issue: command under pressure.

The strikeout rate is the shared positive. 21 strikeouts across 13.0 innings (1.62 K/IP) confirms that both pitchers have enough on the ball to miss bats when they are in the zone. This is the foundation that makes the command work worth doing. A pitcher who cannot miss bats at all has a lower ceiling. Both of these pitchers have a meaningful ceiling if they can elevate Strike% into the 57–62% range consistently.

The combined ERA of 11.77 is the output of everything above. It is not driven by contact — combined WHIP with hits only (ignoring walks) would look much better. The runs are coming from free baserunners who then score, either on contact or on additional wild pitches. Fix the command, the ERA follows.

What a shared development plan looks like

Priority 1 — First pitch strikes. Both pitchers need a singular focus on getting ahead 0-1 rather than falling behind 1-0. First pitch strike percentage drives everything downstream: fewer pitches per batter, fewer walks, more strikeouts in favorable counts, and lower pitch counts that allow longer outings. Every bullpen session should track first pitch strikes as the primary metric.

Priority 2 — Eliminate the walk-then-wild-pitch sequence. The most damaging pattern in both profiles is walk followed by wild pitch — a free baserunner immediately advanced one base at no cost. This combination scores runs without requiring a single hit. Mechanical consistency in the delivery is the root fix.

Priority 3 — Low-leverage deployment until Strike% stabilizes. Neither pitcher should be used in close games or high-leverage situations until Strike% is consistently above 55% across at least three consecutive outings. Track the metric game by game and use it as the objective threshold for increased responsibility, not feel or outcome.

Combined bottom line — Two pitchers, one command profile. The shared problem is walks and wild pitches at a rate that creates free runs before the defense gets involved. The shared asset is a strikeout rate that proves the arm is there. Paul A shows more recent improvement in WP/IP and K/BB trend. Tripp A has a limited sample that makes him harder to evaluate but the STORM outing was a significant red flag against competitive opposition. Individually or combined, the development path is the same: first pitch strikes, mechanical consistency, and patient deployment in low-leverage situations while the work progresses.
